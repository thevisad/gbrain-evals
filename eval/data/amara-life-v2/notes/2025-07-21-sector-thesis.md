Been sitting with a nagging question all week: are we underweighting the compute layer in our climate thesis, or overweighting it?

Had a long walk-and-talk with [Marcus Reid](people/marcus-reid) yesterday after his panel at the infrastructure summit. He pushed back on our framing — argues we're too quick to treat AI inference costs as a liability for climate-adjacent workloads when the optimization gains often net positive within 18 months. I think he's right, but only for a narrow slice of use cases. Grid balancing and demand forecasting fit. Carbon accounting pipelines, less obviously so.

What I keep returning to: the real constraint isn't compute efficiency, it's data provenance. If the underlying grid telemetry is noisy or siloed, no amount of clever inference architecture fixes the output. That feels like where we should be sharpening our diligence.

Marcus and I agreed to map this out properly before our Q3 LP update. Want a cleaner framework than what we presented in the spring.
